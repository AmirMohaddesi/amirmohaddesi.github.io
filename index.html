About

Let me introduce myself.

I began my academic journey by winning a silver medal in the National Physics Olympiad Contest in Iran during my high school years. This achievement inspired me to pursue higher education at Sharif University of Technology, where I graduated in 2019 with a B.S. in Computer Engineering and a cumulative GPA of 3.95.
I then joined the University of California, Irvine, for my Ph.D. in Information & Computer Science, maintaining a cumulative GPA of 3.93.
My research has significantly contributed to the field of autonomous systems and robot navigation. My initial project involved improving the autonomous navigation of the Toyota Human Support Robot (HSR) by mapping the entire floor of a building using SLAM in ROS.
This research also led to the development of a Python QT graphical user interface (GUI) that remarkably reduced cognitive load for human operators, experimented with 22 human subjects during a scavenger hunt task.
I progressed to investigating cognitive load in telepresence robots, where my study 'Navigation and Cognitive Load in Telepresence Robots' revealed that autonomous features in telepresence robots could significantly reduce cognitive load and facilitate user operation.
Further advancing my research, my publication 'Benefit of Varying Navigation Strategies in Robot Teams' explores the impact of mixed navigation strategies on task completion time, environment exploration, and system effectiveness in multi-robot systems.
Utilizing the Webots simulation environment, we demonstrated substantial improvements in robot coordination and efficiency. My current focus involves evaluating decentralized mapping techniques and their robustness against communication loss, aiming to enhance the reliability and effectiveness of autonomous systems in dynamic and unpredictable environments
Out of work, I love to spend time with our kittens, go camping, hike and play video games!

Benefits of Varying Navigation Strategies in Robot Teams

Established a Webots simulation of a maze-shaped art exhibition with multi-agent Clearpath PR2 robots and investigated the benefits of employing varying cognitive navigation strategies inspired by human studies. A robust multi-agent simulation was achieved using unique obstacle avoidance C++ controllers with pairwise robot communications. Explored strategies including Route, Survey, and mixed approaches, highlighting the advantages of variability in navigation strategies for improved task completion time and environment coverage. Presented at From animals to animats 17, SAB2024.

Navigation and Cognitive Load in Telepresence Robots

Telepresence robots have become integrated into educational, medical, and workplace settings as the capabilities of remote technologies progress. Considering the recent pandemic, the deployment of telepresence robots to digitally represent individuals in remote environments has become more important. The goal of this study was to investigate if implementing autonomous features, such as navigation, in telepresence robots could reduce cognitive load and facilitate operation for the user. While many studies involving telepresence robots examine navigation, there has been little research on how different types of navigation affect cognitive load and user experience. The present study measured differences between autonomous and manual navigation on cognitive load, spatial awareness, presence, and user experience during a scavenger hunt task using a telepresence robot. We found that in the autonomous navigation condition, participants moved around their environment more efficiently, performed better in a learning and memory task, and had lower cognitive load assessments than those in the manual navigation condition. These results suggest that incorporating autonomous navigation functions into telepresence robots may lessen cognitive load, enabling individuals to attend to more stimuli in their environment and improve learning in educational settings. Presented at IEEE ICDL24.
